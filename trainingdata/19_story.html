Bob Neumeier back at work months after stroke

The Boston Globe Share Tweet

By Chad Finn Globe Staff  May 01, 2015

One of the tricks to a successful career as a sports television host and reporter is to make it look effortless. Live television is chaos. Those who convince the camera with casual smoothness that all is operating according to plan will never lack for work.

It’s a trick Bob Neumeier — “Neumy” to those who know him personally or, viscerally, as a long-familiar and well-liked personality — mastered long ago. During his various and varied gigs — sports anchor at Channel 4, horse racing expert on NBC, midday host at WEEI, and since 2010, a Comcast SportsNet New England host — he has been a never-let-’em-see-you-sweat type.

Neumy? He could find the one-liner to diffuse any technical difficulty.

But Tuesday, in preparation for the 6 p.m. “SportsNet Central” program, Neumeier was nervous. No, it did not show — he was as polished as ever — but the butterflies were there.

With good reason, too. Tuesday marked his first on-air appearance since suffering a stroke in October, one that required 5½ hours of surgery.

“I was really nervous,’’ said Neumeier during a conversation Thursday, his first extensive public comments on what he endured. “I didn’t think there would be (butterflies), but there were. There’s a lot of people involved here, colleagues and cohorts. They have an eye out, to see how I’m doing. Viewers have an eye out. People are saying, ‘What is he doing? What does he sound like? What does he look like?’ I didn’t want it to be a freak show, not that it would have been. I just wanted it to be seamless. Maybe they missed me, maybe they didn’t, but I wanted [viewers to think] my work would be the same. Maybe even better. That was how I thought about it.”

Neumeier’s return came without much fanfare, which was by design.

‘I don’t want to see the brain scan. It’s right there on the computer, but I don’t want to see what happened . . . I had a surgeon operating on my brain for six hours. One false move and I’m gone. What else is there to know?’

Bob Neumeier, on his stroke last fall 

“There were no press releases, there were no ‘he’s back,’ no blaring of trumpets and clarion calls,’’ he said with a laugh. “No ‘he’s back in the saddle.’ I just wanted to show up and do my job. Hopefully, people liked it. That’s how I wanted to do it. Experience helps. I’ve done it a long time. Once you’re out there and just get through the first section, you can exhale a little bit. But I was really nervous. I didn’t know how it would go. From all indications, it went pretty well.”

Neumeier will for now anchor three days per week on “SportsNet Central,” including Friday’s 6 p.m. program. He will work on the 6 p.m. show for now, working up to the 10:30 program and the other, more complex shows he has hosted in the past.

“The 6 p.m. show is more scripted, and easier for me,’’ he said. “Ten-thirty, a little less so. ‘The Baseball Show’ and ‘Sports Tonight,’ we just wing it, by and large. I say that in all honesty. That was the plan going in and we’ve kind of stuck to it.”

Neumeier has recorded a segment for NBC’s coverage of the Kentucky Derby this weekend. He was hoping to do more but decided not to push it. He will return to his full role as one of the network’s horse racing experts during the Preakness Stakes.

It was in advance of another horse racing event when Neumeier was stricken. In late October, he was preparing to head to Los Angeles for the Breeders’ Cup, which takes place the first Saturday in November.

“I didn’t get on that plane because I was sick as a dog,’’ he said. “I thought I had food poisoning. I told my wife, ‘I can’t get on that plane.’

“Next thing I know, it’s a week later, and I’m in Mass. General with a tube sticking out of my mouth and Michelle looking over at me and saying, ‘You had a stroke.’ ’’

Neumeier has no recollection of the stroke or the surrounding moments.

Details of his status and progress were kept private even from longtime colleagues and friends. But the overwhelming and genuine concern for his well-being never wavered.

CSNNE set up a page for viewers to provide well wishes, and a day rarely passed at this address in which I did not receive concerned queries about Neumeier’s condition or requests to pass along a get-well-soon wish.

“I think by and large people respect what I do,” he said. “I hope so. I put a lot of time into it. I want to make it better than the average job. I think people appreciate that. This was an experience I didn’t ask for. It just happened.”

Neumeier said the stroke occurred in his lateral cerebellum. His balance and coordination was affected, but not his motor skills or speech. He had to learn to dress and shower all over again, and he said his left shoulder is “frozen,’’ though it’s uncertain whether that is an effect of the stroke.

“It doesn’t affect me unless I’m putting on my jacket,’’ he said. “I’ve graduated from a walker to a cane to walking pretty well on my own. But right now, a gust of wind could blow me over if I’m walking on the streets. It’s a gradual process. I get tired. I get headaches.”

Neumeier has met with several neurologists during his recovery. He’s chatted at length with Dr. Aman B. Patel, who performed the surgery. But he said he has no interest in learning the details of what happened to him.

“I don’t want to revisit,’’ he said. “I don’t want to see the brain scan. It’s right there on the computer, but I don’t want to see what happened. I don’t want to see what caused it. I don’t want any part of it.

“I had a surgeon operating on my brain for six hours. One false move and I’m gone. What else is there to know?”

“That’s why getting back is emotional,’’ added Neumeier. “That’s why you get nerves and appreciate things you might have taken for granted. My boss is rooting for me and my wife — she has been such a rock through all of this — is rooting for me and my colleagues are rooting for me and friends and viewers and I don’t know what to say but thanks. I say thanks. Thanks. I’m keeping on.”
